
Title : To a Mountain Daisy <br>
Author : Robert Burns <br><br>

On Turning One Down with the Plow, in April, 1786<br>
Wee, modest, crimson-tipped <mark4>flow'r,</mark4><br>
Thou's met me in an evil <mark4>hour;</mark4><br>
For I maun crush amang the <mark5>stoure</mark5><br>
Thy slender <mark3>stem:</mark3><br>
To spare thee now is past my <mark4>pow'r,</mark4><br>
Thou bonie <mark3>gem.</mark3><br>
<br>
Alas! it's no thy neibor <mark3>sweet,</mark3><br>
The bonie lark, companion <mark3>meet,</mark3><br>
Bending thee 'mang the dewy weet <br>
Wi' spreck'd <mark4>breast,</mark4><br>
When upward-springing, blythe, to <mark4>greet</mark4><br>
The purpling <mark4>east.</mark4><br>
<br>
Cauld blew the bitter-biting <mark4>north</mark4><br>
Upon thy early, humble <mark4>birth;</mark4><br>
Yet cheerfully thou glinted <mark4>forth</mark4><br>
Amid the <mark4>storm,</mark4><br>
Scarce rear'd above the <mark4>parent-earth</mark4><br>
Thy tender <mark3>form.</mark3><br>
<br>
The flaunting flowers our gardens <mark3>yield</mark3><br>
High shelt'ring woods an' wa's maun <mark3>shield:</mark3><br>
But thou, beneath the random bield <br>
O' clod or <mark5>stane,</mark5><br>
Adorns the histie <mark3>stibble-field</mark3><br>
<mark4>Unseen</mark4>, <mark4>alane.</mark4><br>
<br>
There, in thy scanty mantle <mark4>clad,</mark4><br>
Thy snawie-bosom sun-ward <mark4>spread,</mark4><br>
Thou lifts thy unassuming <mark3>head</mark3><br>
In humble <mark4>guise;</mark4><br>
But now the share uptears thy <mark3>bed,</mark3><br>
And low thou <mark3>lies!</mark3><br>
<br>
Such is the fate of artless <mark4>maid,</mark4><br>
Sweet flow'ret of the rural <mark3>shade!</mark3><br>
By love's simplicity <mark4>betray'd</mark4><br>
And guileless <mark4>trust;</mark4><br>

Low i' the <mark3>dust.</mark3><br>
<br>
Such is the fate of simple <mark4>bard,</mark4><br>
On life's rough ocean luckless <mark4>starr'd!</mark4><br>
Unskilful he to note the <mark3>card</mark3><br>
Of prudent <mark4>lore,</mark4><br>
Till billows rage and gales blow <mark3>hard,</mark3><br>
And whelm him <mark4>o'er!</mark4><br>
<br>
Such fate to suffering Worth is giv'n, <br>
Who long with wants and woes has striv'n, <br>
By human pride or cunning driv'n <br>
To mis'ry's <mark3>brink;</mark3><br>
Till, wrench'd of ev'ry stay but Heav'n, <br>
He ruin'd <mark3>sink!</mark3><br>
<br>
Ev'n thou who mourn'st the Daisy's <mark4>fate,</mark4><br>
That fate is thine--no distant <mark3>date;</mark3><br>
Stern Ruin's ploughshare drives <mark3>elate,</mark3><br>
Full on thy <mark4>bloom,</mark4><br>
Till crush'd beneath the furrow's <mark3>weight</mark3><br>
Shall be thy <mark3>doom.</mark3><br>


Title : From Lines to William Simson <br>
Author : Robert Burns <br><br>

Auld Coila now may fidge fu' <mark3>fain,</mark3><br>
She's gotten poets o' her ain-- <br>
Chiels wha their chanters winna <mark3>hain,</mark3><br>
But tune their <mark3>lays,</mark3><br>
Till echoes a' resound <mark3>again</mark3><br>
Her weel-sung <mark3>praise.</mark3><br>
<br>
Nae poet thought her worth his <mark3>while</mark3><br>
To set her name in measur'd <mark3>style:</mark3><br>
She lay like some unken'd-of <mark3>isle</mark3><br>
Beside New <mark4>Holland,</mark4><br>
Or whare wild-meeting oceans <mark4>boil</mark4><br>
Besouth <mark4>Magellan.</mark4><br>
<br>
Ramsay and famous <mark4>Fergusson</mark4><br>
Gied Forth and Tay a lift aboon; <br>
Yarrow and Tweed to mony a <mark4>tune</mark4><br>
Owre Scotland <mark4>rings;</mark4><br>
While Irvin, Lugar, Ayr an' Doon <br>
Naebody <mark3>sings.</mark3><br>
<br>
Th' Ilissus, Tiber, Thames, an' <mark4>Seine</mark4><br>
Glide sweet in mony a tunefu' <mark4>line;</mark4><br>
But, Willie, set your fit to <mark3>mine</mark3><br>
And cock your <mark3>crest,</mark3><br>
We'll gar our streams and burnies <mark3>shine</mark3><br>
Up wi' the <mark3>best!</mark3><br>
<br>
We'll sing auld Coila's plains an' <mark4>fells,</mark4><br>
Her moors red-brown wi' heather <mark3>bells,</mark3><br>
Her banks an' braes, her dens an' dells, <br>
            Where glorious Wallace <br>
Aft bure the gree, as story <mark3>tells,</mark3><br>
            Frae Southron billies. <br>
<br>
At Wallace' name what Scottish <mark4>blood</mark4><br>
But boils up in a spring-tide <mark3>flood!</mark3><br>
Oft have our fearless fathers <mark4>strode</mark4><br>
By Wallace' <mark4>side,</mark4><br>
Still pressing onward <mark4>red-wat-shod,</mark4><br>
            Or glorious dy'd. <br>
<br>
O sweet are Coila's haughs an' <mark4>woods,.</mark4><br>
When lintwhites chant amang the <mark4>buds,</mark4><br>
And jinkin hares in amorous <mark5>whids</mark5><br>
            Their loves enjoy, <br>
While thro' the braes the cushat croods <br>
            Wi' wailfu' cry! <br>
<br>
Ev'n winter bleak has charms to <mark3>me,</mark3><br>
When winds rave thro' the naked <mark3>tree;</mark3><br>
Or frosts on hills of Ochiltree <br>
Are hoary <mark3>gray;</mark3><br>
Or blinding drifts wild-furious <mark3>flee,</mark3><br>
Dark'ning the <mark3>day!</mark3><br>
<br>
O Nature! a' thy shews an' <mark4>forms</mark4><br>

Whether the summer kindly <mark4>warms</mark4><br>
Wi' life an' <mark4>light,</mark4><br>
Or winter howls in gusty <mark3>storms</mark3><br>
The lang, dark <mark3>night!</mark3><br>
<br>
The Muse, nae poet ever fand her, <br>
Till by himsel he learn'd to <mark4>wander</mark4><br>
Adoun some trottin burn's <mark4>meander,</mark4><br>
And no think <mark3>lang;</mark3><br>
O sweet to stray and pensive <mark4>ponder</mark4><br>
A heart-felt <mark3>sang!</mark3><br>
<br>
The warly race may drudge and <mark3>drive,</mark3><br>
Hog-shouther, jundie, stretch an' <mark3>strive:</mark3><br>
Let me fair nature's face descrive, <br>
And I wi' <mark4>pleasure</mark4><br>
Shall let the busy, grumbling <mark3>hive</mark3><br>
Bum owre their <mark3>treasure.</mark3><br>
